MOST LIKELY United more likely wins the patent; Liquidia more likely survives it.

Liability — P(United wins) ≈ 60%. Infringement is conceded, so this is a pure validity fight, and Liquidia has to win one of two invalidity attacks. On all our data, both attacks are uphill in this specific courtroom: obviousness runs against a judge whose record consistently upholds treatment-method claims, and the on-sale bar — though the judge will likely find sales occurred — fails on the legal layer United controls (a product sale is not a method sale; the only sales were confidential third-party pharmacy transactions; the "use" theory that would bridge the gap was struck pretrial). Stack the patentee-favorable burden (clear-and-convincing; ties go to United) on top, and Liquidia is the side that must be clearly right twice. It probably isn't. The "coin-flip" framing was an artifact of Liquidia getting two swings — but two uphill swings don't add to even.

Remedy — conditional on a United win, the most likely result is a skinny label, not a market withdrawal. The '327 covers PH-ILD only; United's own filing concedes the infringement is "due to its PH-ILD indication," Liquidia has a documented carve-out path (Ferring) and a separately-approved PAH label, and Andrews' patient-harm equities cut against pulling the drug. So a United patent win most likely means Liquidia loses PH-ILD and keeps the larger PAH franchise. The feared whole-product interruption is a real but secondary tail (~15%), and even it would most likely be temporary and stayed on appeal.

Net trade-relevant read: United is the more likely patent winner, and Liquidia the more likely business survivor. The single ruling that could move the liability conclusion is the on-sale legal question (§3); the evidence there resolves to United, which is why the headline lands where it does — and it is the one node to watch for a change.

1. Where the case stands

In simple terms The trial is finished but the judge hasn't written his decision yet, so this is a forecast. Yutrepia is already approved and on the market for two uses — the big one (PAH) and the smaller one this patent covers (PH-ILD). Because patients are already on the drug, the judge has middle-ground options, not just "block it or don't." That single fact is why even a loss for Liquidia is most likely survivable.

This is a live handicap of a decision that has not issued. From the primary record:

  • LQDA FY2025 10-K (filed 5 Mar 2026): "Trial was held in June 2025. Post-trial briefing has been completed, and the court's decision is pending. The trial was limited to determining whether YUTREPIA infringes any valid claims of the '327 patent."
  • The D. Del. public-opinions folder for 23-975 holds only 2024 opinions (PI denial, Markman, two discovery orders) — no post-trial Findings of Fact.
  • No litigation 8-K from LQDA since the 10-K; a merits ruling triggers an immediate Item 8.01, and its absence corroborates "pending." The 8 Jun 2026 8-K still markets Yutrepia for both PAH and PH-ILD → no injunction has touched the label.

Yutrepia is an FDA-approved, launched 505(b)(2) product (mid-2025), not a pre-launch Hatch-Waxman / 30-month-stay posture. That is why the realistic adverse remedy is a label carve-out, not a market clearing — and why the equities have shifted toward Liquidia since the 2024 PI record.

2. The whole case is one question: is claim 1 valid?

In simple terms Liquidia already admitted its drug does what the patent describes, so the only fight left is whether the patent is good. Everything rides on one foundational claim ("claim 1"); the rest are built on top of it. If claim 1 holds, United wins automatically; if it's knocked out, United is left with weaker backups. And because the law presumes a granted patent is valid, a tie goes to United — Liquidia has to win clearly.

The '327 patent ("Treatment for Interstitial Lung Disease") claims methods of treating PH-ILD with inhaled treprostinil. Asserted claims are 1, 5, 6, 9, 14, 17 (claim 11 appears only as claim 14's antecedent). The structure is decisive:

  • Infringement of claims 1 and 14 is STIPULATED (Tr. 28). Every asserted claim depends from claim 1. So this is a pure validity contest.
  • Claim 1 valid → United wins liability outright. A claim depending from a valid independent claim is itself valid, and claim 1 is already infringed by stipulation.
  • Claim 1 invalid → United must rescue a dependent claim alone — claim 14 (the DPI limitation, §6) or 5/6/9/17 (the Bayer "results" claims, §7), both weaker.

Claim 1 faces exactly two live invalidity theories — on-sale bar and obviousness (inherent anticipation is dead, §5). Liquidia needs only one to land; United needs to survive both. My call: United survives both more likely than not — laid out next.

3. On-sale bar — Liquidia's biggest swing, and I score it for United

In simple terms You can't patent something already sold for that use before you filed. Liquidia says doctors were already prescribing United's older drug to these patients years earlier. The judge signaled he'll probably find those sales happened — which sounds bad for United. But selling a drug isn't the same as selling the patented method of using it, and the judge openly said he wasn't sure the two connect. That gap is where United wins this.

This is the closest, highest-variance node in the case — and the one where the judge's track record is silent (his prior treprostinil case ran on anticipation, obviousness, and §112, never a §102(b) on-sale theory). He tipped his factual lean from the bench:

Judge Andrews — Trial Tr. ~995 (Day 4 closing) If I had to say, I think I heard enough evidence to be convinced that there were pharmacies that were filling … Tyvaso prescriptions where the doctors had written them to treat PH-ILD … the patients weren't getting this for free. So as a factual matter, I think I'm going to find that there were sales … There may be things on the periphery that mean they're not sales that count, but I think there were sales by pharmacies for sure.

Read the hedge — "sales that count." The judge himself, unprompted, flagged the legal question that decides this, and admitted he had no authority for it:

Judge Andrews — Trial Tr. ~976–977 The sale by itself is not practicing the method … where there's a prescription written and then a doctor goes off and treats PH-ILD with it … What's needed to connect those two things that will make that an anticipatory commercial sale? … sometimes I think of issues that are not issues, [but] I would like to have a case that says what you just said.
Why I score this for United

Split the question into its two layers and United wins the one that matters:

  • Fact layer (did sales occur?) — Liquidia. The judge will likely find pharmacies sold Tyvaso to PH-ILD patients pre-cutoff. Concede it.
  • Legal layer (do those sales count?) — United, and this is the layer that controls. Three independent reasons, and United only needs one:
    • The procedural knockout (strongest). Andrews struck Liquidia's prior-use defense pretrial, leaving only prior-sale. But a pharmacy selling a vial doesn't practice the method — to bridge "sale" to "method" Liquidia needs a public-use showing, which it waived. And the only sales were confidential, third-party, HIPAA-protected pharmacy transactions, which under W.L. Gore are not a §102 bar. United can win on-sale as a matter of law, without the judge ever weighing the facts.
    • BASF "product ≠ method." A sale of multi-use Tyvaso (whose only approved pre-2019 use was PAH — which Liquidia concedes is not claimed) is not a sale of the claimed PH-ILD method.
    • Not "ready for patenting." The PH-ILD efficacy wasn't established until INCREASE unblinded in 2020, after the April 2019 critical date.

The judge's own words point United's way: a judge who says "the sale by itself is not practicing the method" and asks Liquidia for a case that closes the gap is signaling he sees the gap. Layer the burden (Liquidia must prove this by clear-and-convincing) on top, and the rational call is ~40% Liquidia / 60% United on this node — close, but United-favored. The one construction that helps Liquidia (the claim reads on PH "due, at least in part, to ILD," DE393, so an insurance code reading "PAH" may not save United) is real and is why this isn't a blowout — but it goes to the facts, and United wins on the law above it.

4. Obviousness — I score it for United, and the judge's record is why

In simple terms Liquidia's other attack is that the invention was an obvious next step from what doctors already knew. On the bare record it's close. But this exact judge has a long, consistent history of upholding treatment-method patents like this one against obviousness, and only striking down narrow "manufacturing-recipe" patents. So this is the weaker of Liquidia's two shots, not a co-equal one.

Liquidia's combination is Faria-Urbina 2018 + the '793 patent (+ Saggar 2014 for the dependents). The trial record genuinely cuts both ways:

  • Helps United: six prior double-blind trials of PAH drugs in PH-ILD had failed (two stopped early for harm); per United's proposed findings, '793 inventor Dr. Rubin had publicly said treating PH-ILD "does not work"; the 6th World Symposium cautioned against it. Andrews got Liquidia to concede the field was "controversial" (Tr. 968–969) and agreed the off-label prescribers were elite specialists, not the ordinary POSA — "I assumed Duke and UCLA and Mass General … are leaders in the field" (Tr. 986).
  • Helps Liquidia: those specialists were already using inhaled treprostinil in these patients pre-priority; Dr. Hill testified Faria-Urbina discloses claim 1's dosing, and the dosing overlap wasn't seriously contested; and Liquidia turns United's own prior admission (that '793 met a "long-felt need" for treating PH) into a no-nexus argument against United's secondary considerations.
Judge layer — this is why obviousness is the weaker shot

Andrews is a known quantity on exactly this doctrine-and-claim-type combination, and it points to United:

  • His district record protects method/formulation claims. Across 11 verified merits decisions he rejected obviousness (patentee won) on compound/formulation/method claims — Orexigen/Contrave, Sanofi/Multaq, Impax/Zomig, Endo/Opana, Pfizer/Rapamune, Novartis/Afinitor, plus the surviving '323 claim in Merck/Invanz — and reserved obviousness invalidation for process / routine-optimization claims (Merck '150, Hospira/Precedex). The '327 is a treatment-method/results claim. It sits in his patentee-protective zone.
  • His non-obviousness findings stick on appeal. Nalpropion, Impax, West-Ward all affirmed; his overall affirmance rate is ~88% (7 of 8), and his single reversal (Breckenridge) overturned an invalidation on a legal term-doctrine — the appellate crack runs against invalidations, not patentee wins.
  • The §112 lane is dead in his court. Written-description and enablement defenses were rejected every time in his sample, and his §112 reasoning on the predecessor '793 was affirmed in full (74 F.4th 1360) in this very dispute.

The one caveat — and it does not change this node. The predecessor '793 inhalation patent Andrews upheld at trial was later killed on obviousness by the PTAB under its lower preponderance standard — so obviousness is genuinely lethal to treprostinil-inhalation claims, but historically at the PTAB, not at Andrews' bench. That is a separate-forum risk to a United win (§9), not a reason Liquidia wins obviousness at this trial. At Andrews' bench, the rational call is ~35% Liquidia / 65% United.

5. Inherent anticipation — dead (United)

In simple terms Liquidia's third, most technical attack is a dead end. From the bench the judge called himself "dubious" about it and called the version aimed at the backup claims "pretty close to frivolous." That matches his long record, where this kind of defense has essentially never worked. So Liquidia is really down to its two real weapons — and I've scored both for United.
Judge Andrews — Trial Tr. ~1002 The reason why I'm sort of dubious about all the inherent anticipation is none of this is leading to something that you can say as a scientific matter that it's going to happen. We're not in the world of hard science, we're in the world of probability, statistics, and just randomness. So I'm pretty dubious on this.

Consistent with his 2024 ruling rejecting anticipation here, and with his broader record (0 anticipation invalidations in the 11-case sample). On the dependent-claim version he was sharper still — "pretty close to frivolous" (Tr. 635). Score it United.

Myth-check The circulated claim that "Andrews said he did not believe United's expert (Dr. Nathan)" is not in the record — it is a misreading of the "dubious" line, which is about Liquidia's inherent-anticipation theory, not Nathan's credibility.

6. Claim 14 (DPI) — only matters if claim 1 falls, and even then it's a toss-up

In simple terms This is United's backup, live only if claim 1 is knocked out. Claim 14 covers using a dry-powder inhaler specifically; United's best move is a technicality — getting the older "'793" patent thrown out because United owned both patents. Liquidia has fallback evidence even without it, and the dry-powder idea was already admitted to be well-known. So this backup is a coin-toss — but it only comes up in the 40% world where United has already lost claim 1.

Claim 14 = claim 1 + a dry-powder inhaler; infringement stipulated, so pure validity. United argues the '793 cannot be prior art at all under the §102(b)(2)(C) common-ownership exception: it issued 21 Jul 2020, after the '327's 17 Apr 2020 priority date (so it is §102(a)(2) art, not §102(a)(1) — both sides agree), and both patents were commonly owned, so it "shall not be prior art." Liquidia's counter is procedural (its Motion to Strike says United sandbagged the theory via a late interrogatory supplement) plus fallbacks if '793 is excluded: the '507 patent (same spec, issued May 2016 → §102(a)(1), not barred), applicant-admitted prior art, and collateral estoppel from the prior '793 case where United argued (and this Court found, affirmed) a treprostinil DPI was well-known by 2006. Andrews deferred it all: "why don't we just assume it's prior art and move on" (Tr. 957).

Call On the merits the DPI is obvious (United already conceded the art), so United's only path is the procedural knockout of '793 plus the waiver of the '507 substitute. Andrews dislikes sandbagging — which cuts against United's late common-ownership disclosure. I score claim 14 a true toss-up (~50/50) — but it is downstream of a claim-1 loss, so it carries little weight in the headline. It is the thin thread by which United could still win even in the 40% world where claim 1 falls.

7. The dependent "results" claims fall with claim 1 (Bayer v. Mylan)

In simple terms United's other backups describe the medical results of the treatment (better walking distance, fewer flare-ups). Liquidia set a trap with a 2025 appeals ruling: if a results claim adds nothing to the basic method, it falls along with claim 1. United is stuck, because to prove infringement it argued these results happen automatically — which is exactly what makes them count for nothing on validity. So if claim 1 dies, these die with it.

Claims 5/6/9/17 (NT-proBNP, ILD exacerbations, FVC, 6MWD) are United's fallback if claim 1 falls. Using United's own infringement position — that these require no measurement and are "automatically infringed" whenever claim 1 is performed — Liquidia argues that under the Federal Circuit's Sept-2025 Bayer v. Mylan, a results limitation that adds nothing to the method gets no patentable weight, so the dependents fall with claim 1. Andrews raised Bayer on his own motion (hence the supplemental letters). United is impaled on its own position — it cannot call the outcomes "automatic" (to prove infringement) yet "functionally narrowing" (to prove validity). Call: if claim 1 falls, 5/6/9/17 most likely fall with it. They only help United in the world where claim 1 already survives — in which case they are moot.

8. Remedy — a United win most likely means a skinny label, not a withdrawal

In simple terms If United wins, how badly does it hurt Liquidia? Most likely: Liquidia drops the smaller PH-ILD use and keeps selling for its big PAH market — a "skinny label." A harsher outcome that briefly freezes the whole product is possible but secondary, probably temporary, and probably paused during appeal. The judge has already shown he doesn't want to take a working drug from patients, and called any win for United "a mess regardless." This is why a patent loss is survivable.

The two sides are far apart on remedy:

  • Liquidia offers a carve-out: file a supplement to "remove the PH-ILD indication and revert to its previously adjudicated approval for PAH-only."
  • United seeks a whole-NDA reset: the mandatory §271(e)(4)(A) order setting Yutrepia's approval to no earlier than the '327's expiry, and — citing Salix v. Norwich (Fed. Cir. 2024) — argues it must attach to the entire infringing application, so PAH is collateral damage.
Judge Andrews — Trial Tr. ~1010 (declining United's fast-track Rule 52(c) bid) My reaction to the 52(c) is like you want a preliminary injunction and long ago that route was pursued … if I rule in your favor on this, it's going to be a mess regardless … you ought to address that in your briefing.
Call — skinny label is the rational base case

Three things point to the carve-out over the whole-NDA reset: (i) United's own form-of-judgment letter concedes infringement is "due to its PH-ILD indication"; (ii) Liquidia has a separately-adjudicated PAH-only approval from the prior case and a documented carve-out path (Ferring v. Watson: amend to a non-infringing product → no §271(e)(4)(A) injunction), distinguishing Salix; and (iii) the injunction piece (§271(e)(4)(B)) runs through eBay, where Andrews' 2024 patient-harm findings — United's harm "too speculative," patients would "suffer negative consequences if Defendant were enjoined" — favor Liquidia, and are stronger now the drug is launched with titrated patients. Conditional on a United win: ~75% skinny label, ~25% whole-NDA interruption — and the latter would most likely be temporary (pending PAH-only re-approval) and stayed pending appeal.

10. Where the bull and bear are wrong

  • Bull (LQDA) is too sanguine. "Andrews will find prior sale and Liquidia wins" conflates the fact with the issue — he reserved the legal question that actually decides it, and that question favors United. The obviousness shot is also softer than the bull case assumes given this judge's method-claim record.
  • Bear (UTHR-disaster) overstates the downside. Even on a United win, the "drug pulled" scenario is ~15%, temporary, and stayable; the base case is a carve-out that leaves PAH intact.
  • The rational center is not a coin-flip. It is United wins the patent (~60%), Liquidia survives it (~85%) — a directional call, not a shrug.

11. The litigation is bigger than '327 — and bidirectional

In simple terms Even a clean result here doesn't end the fight. United has a second patent suit against Liquidia in North Carolina and may bring more; cutting the other way, Liquidia is suing United on its own patent over United's competing inhaler. Size the position for a string of binary events, not one verdict.

(From the FY2025 10-K.) '782 patent — UTHR v. LQDA, M.D.N.C. (Judge Schroeder); PI denied May 2025, transfer denied Nov 2025, MTD denied Dec 2025; live. '494 patent (US 10,898,494) — Liquidia's own; LQDA is plaintiff, alleging UTHR's Tyvaso DPI infringes (M.D.N.C. 1:25-cv-00299); UTHR claims an ownership interest via a former-employee trade-secret theory. Trade-secret suit — UTHR v. LQDA + ex-employee, Durham County (NC), ongoing. '887 patent — flagged as one UTHR "may in the future seek to assert." The longer-term competitive threat (TPIP / L606) may ultimately matter more than the patent docket.

12. The one input that would change the conclusion

In simple terms The analysis points to United winning the patent, so here's the honest single point of failure rather than a list of hedges. If the judge decides those pharmacy sales do legally count as selling the patented method, Liquidia probably wins outright — and that's a question this judge has never faced and asked both sides for help on. Everything else the record resolves clearly enough to state outright.
  1. The on-sale legal ruling is the whole ballgame for the flip. I scored it ~60% United on the product-vs-method / third-party-sale / waiver law. If Andrews instead finds a confidential third-party pharmacy sale of a multi-use drug does anticipate the method, claim 1 likely falls and my 60% United inverts toward Liquidia. This is the one node his ruling history can't calibrate, and the one I'd revisit on any new authority.
  2. Separate-forum tail to a United win: whether the '327 faces a live PTAB/IPR — the lower-standard forum that actually killed the predecessor '793. I have not confirmed one exists; if it does, a United district win is less durable than it looks. (Open item, §13.)
  3. Everything else I'm calling: obviousness (United), inherent anticipation (United, dead), the dependents falling with claim 1, and the skinny-label remedy base case. These are directional calls I'm comfortable committing to on the record we have.
